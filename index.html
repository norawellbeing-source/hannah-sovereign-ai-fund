QUESTIONS YOU MIGHT BE ASKING

A few I'd ask myself

Because programme management and governance work like this is what I actually enjoy doing, not just what I happen to be good at. I like building the structure that lets a team move fast without losing control of what matters. This fund is trying to do that at a genuinely difficult intersection, investment speed and public money discipline, and that's an interesting problem to solve. I already understand it from the inside, having built governance infrastructure for a brand new ISF portfolio at DSIT over the past year.

Yes, and that's deliberate on the Fund's part as much as mine. This role isn't asking for someone who's spent years inside a VC fund, it's asking for someone who can build the governance a public investment programme is legally required to have, while keeping pace with how fast the market moves. I bring the government and regulated-environment side properly: board reporting, audit trails, financial oversight, delegated authority. What I've added on top is my own time spent learning the tools the AI sector actually runs on, and going to find founders directly at events like London Tech Week rather than waiting for the sector to come to me.

Map the governance cycle end to end before assuming anything is missing. I'd want to see how decisions currently move through the Investment Committee and Strategy Board, where the audit trail already exists and where it doesn't, and where commercial, legal and finance colleagues are waiting on each other rather than working in parallel. I did exactly this kind of mapping when I joined my current DSIT role, and the fastest way to slow a fast-moving team down is to start changing things before you understand why they're built the way they are.

Most government governance is built for predictability, not speed. The hard part is keeping the audit trail, the delegated authority and the conflicts of interest register clean enough to survive scrutiny, without adding a process step every time the market moves faster than Whitehall expects. The answer is governance that's lightweight by design, not heavy by default, the kind people don't try to work around because it's actually helping them move.

I haven't worked full-time inside a VC fund, so I won't pretend to have the pattern recognition that comes from seeing hundreds of deals. The closest I've come is evaluating a potential equity investment into a UK biotech startup last year, including due diligence and weighing a Series B round against a convertible loan note, a deal that ultimately didn't proceed. What I bring instead is the governance and delivery infrastructure that sits around decisions like that, built over eight years, and a track record of getting up to speed fast in technical areas I didn't start in.
